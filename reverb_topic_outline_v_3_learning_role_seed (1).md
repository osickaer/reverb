# Reverb Topic Outline

A structured seed outline for generating high-quality Reverb questions.

This version organizes topics by **learning role first** rather than by subject alone. The goal is to help question writers generate prompts that test useful adult knowledge in a clean, repeatable way.

---

## Purpose

Use this outline to decide **what kinds of questions Reverb should generate** before writing the actual question bank.

Reverb is not meant to be random trivia and not meant to feel like school. It should help users become more:

- well-rounded
- conversationally fluent
- comfortable with history, geography, civics, economics, and finance
- capable of following what people mean when they talk about the world

The product goal is not just “don’t be embarrassed.” The better goal is:

**Help users build enough knowledge and mental structure to contribute to real conversations and interpret the world around them with more confidence.**

---

## Organizing model

Use this hierarchy when curating concepts and generating questions:

**Learning role → subtype → concept family → subject/domain → question lens**

### Learning roles for v1

Start with only two top-level learning roles:

1. **Anchor**
2. **Mechanism**

Do not treat **Distinction** as a separate top-level role for now. In most cases, distinction is just a strong **question lens** applied to an anchor or mechanism concept.

Examples:

- APR vs interest rate = mechanism + distinction lens
- House vs Senate = mechanism + distinction lens
- WWII years = anchor + timeline lens
- Where the Strait of Hormuz is = anchor + map lens

### Secondary metadata

Each concept can also carry:

- **subject/domain**: history, geography, civics, government, economics, finance
- **difficulty**: easy, medium, hard
- **question lens**: timeline, map, officeholder, canonical list, distinction, relationship, misconception, process, comparison
- **why it belongs**: what real-world fluency it improves

---

## Core curation rules

Include topics that:

- improve real-world understanding or conversational fluency
- are repeatedly useful, not one-off trivia
- support multiple question angles
- can be explained clearly after the answer
- have enough structure for plausible answer choices

---

## How question writers should use this outline

Each concept should usually be written with a **default question shape** in mind.

### Anchor question shape

Anchor questions should test whether the user can quickly:

- place something in time
- locate something on a map
- identify a leader tied to an event
- recall a canonical reference item

These should usually be clean and direct.

Common lenses:

- timeline
- map
- officeholder
- canonical list

### Mechanism question shape

Mechanism questions should test whether the user understands:

- how a system works

- how two parts relate

- what changes when one variable changes

- common misconceptions

- how two similar-seeming things differ

These should usually require reasoning, not just recognition.

Common lenses:

- distinction
- process
- relationship
- comparison

### Explanation rule for all questions

Each question should later support:

- **1 sentence** explaining why the answer is correct
- **1 sentence** for why it matters or what bigger concept it connects to

# Learning Role 1: Anchor

## Definition

**Anchor concepts are foundational reference points that a well-rounded adult should be able to place, locate, identify, or recall quickly.**

These are low-friction, high-coverage concepts that create mental scaffolding for deeper learning later.

## Anchor subtypes

1. Time anchors
2. Place anchors
3. Officeholder / leadership anchors
4. Canonical list anchors

---

## A. Time anchors

### Purpose

Help users place major events, eras, and transitions in rough chronological order.

### What strong questions look like

- ask when something happened
- ask which period came earlier/later
- ask which event belongs to a rough era
- avoid over-precision unless the exact years truly matter

### Concept families

#### Ancient / classical anchors

- Ancient Egypt and early river civilizations
- Ancient Greece
- Roman Republic and Roman Empire
- Fall of the Western Roman Empire
- Rise and spread of Islam
- Middle Ages / medieval period
- Black Death
- Renaissance
- Age of Exploration

#### Early modern / revolutionary anchors

- American Revolution
- French Revolution
- Industrial Revolution

#### U.S. and modern world anchors

- U.S. Civil War
- World War I
- Great Depression
- World War II
- Cold War
- Civil Rights Movement
- Vietnam War
- Russian Revolution
- Great Recession
- 9/11

### Question writer notes

- Prefer rough but meaningful time orientation over obscure date trivia
- Good answer choices should force the user to truly place the event in time
- Strong distractors usually come from nearby eras, not random centuries

---

## B. Place anchors

### Purpose

Help users know where important places, regions, and landmarks are.

### What strong questions look like

- ask where something is
- ask what state/country/region something belongs to
- ask what two places are adjacent or connected
- ask map fluency questions cleanly without making them too easy through bad answer choices

### Concept families

#### U.S. map fluency

- All 50 U.S. states
- U.S. state capitals
- Major U.S. regions and subregions
- Major U.S. cities and their states
- Great Lakes
- Major U.S. rivers, especially the Mississippi system
- Major U.S. mountain ranges: Rockies, Appalachians, Sierra Nevada

#### World map fluency

- Major countries: Canada, Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, UK, France, Germany, Russia, China, India, Japan, South Korea, Australia, South Africa, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Turkey
- Europe fundamentals
- Middle East fundamentals
- East Asia fundamentals
- North America fundamentals
- South America fundamentals

#### Bodies of water and physical landmarks

- Atlantic, Pacific, Indian, Arctic, Southern oceans
- Mediterranean Sea, Black Sea, Red Sea, South China Sea, Persian Gulf
- Nile, Amazon, Danube, Yangtze, Ganges
- Himalayas, Andes, Alps, Rockies, Appalachians

#### Global chokepoints and trade geography

- Suez Canal
- Panama Canal
- Strait of Hormuz
- Strait of Malacca
- Bosporus

#### Ancient / iconic landmarks

- Great Pyramid of Giza
- Great Wall of China
- Colosseum
- Machu Picchu
- Petra
- Taj Mahal
- Stonehenge

### Question writer notes

- Map questions should test true location fluency, not just elimination
- Strong distractors should be geographically plausible
- Ancient wonder / landmark questions can ask where it is, what civilization it is associated with, or rough era if appropriate

---

## C. Officeholder / leadership anchors

### Purpose

Help users tie major events to the key leader in office at the time.

### Important note

This is **not** meant to become random leader trivia. It should stay attached to major events, eras, and institutional memory.

### What strong questions look like

- ask who was U.S. president during a major event
- ask which leader is most associated with a defining crisis or era
- sometimes ask non-U.S. leaders when their role is globally important

### Concept families

#### U.S. presidents tied to major events or policies

- Abraham Lincoln and the Civil War / Emancipation
- Franklin D. Roosevelt and the Great Depression / New Deal / most of WWII
- Harry Truman and the end of WWII / early Cold War / Marshall Plan era
- Dwight D. Eisenhower and the Interstate Highway System / early Cold War order
- John F. Kennedy and the Cuban Missile Crisis / New Frontier era
- Lyndon B. Johnson and Vietnam / Great Society / Civil Rights era legislation
- Richard Nixon and détente / opening to China / Watergate era
- Ronald Reagan and the late Cold War / Reaganomics
- George W. Bush and 9/11 / War on Terror
- Barack Obama and the Affordable Care Act / post-financial-crisis era

#### Important non-U.S. leaders when useful

- Winston Churchill and WWII
- Joseph Stalin and WWII / early Cold War
- Mikhail Gorbachev and the end of the Cold War

### Question writer notes

- Keep this tied to events or policies users should know anyway
- Avoid turning this into memorizing long lists of officeholders with no real-world payoff
- This lane works best as reinforcement for time anchors

---

## D. Canonical list anchors

### Purpose

Help users know a small number of fixed, foundational reference items that come up constantly.

### What strong questions look like

- ask what a key amendment protects
- ask which branch a role belongs to
- ask which item is part of a foundational list
- ask which right / institution / category matches a known reference point

### Concept families

#### U.S. constitutional anchors

- First Amendment
- Second Amendment
- Fourth Amendment
- Fifth Amendment
- Fourteenth Amendment
- Three branches of government

#### Optional but acceptable if used carefully

- Bill of Rights as a whole
- Foundational founding documents at a high level

---

# Learning Role 2: Mechanism

## Definition

**Mechanism concepts explain how a system, institution, market, or relationship actually works — especially where many adults hold weak or mistaken mental models.**

This is the highest upside lane for helping users become more fluent in intelligent conversation.

## Mechanism subtypes

1. Government and institutional mechanisms
2. Economic mechanisms
3. Finance and investing mechanisms
4. Relationship / variable-change mechanisms

---

## A. Government and institutional mechanisms

### Purpose

Help users understand how political and civic systems actually function.

### What strong questions look like

- ask what each institution does
- ask how powers differ
- ask what limits exist
- ask what process governs a decision
- ask distinction questions where confusion is common

### Concept families

#### Branches and roles

- What the House does
- What the Senate does
- What the president does
- What the Supreme Court does
- Separation of powers and checks and balances

#### Structural distinctions

- House vs Senate
- federal vs state power
- mayor vs governor
- constitutional right vs ordinary policy preference
- department vs independent agency at a high level
- regulator vs science / service agency at a high level

#### Process mechanisms

- how a bill becomes law
- judicial review
- executive orders and their limits
- impeachment: what it is and what it is not
- administrative agencies and how they fit into government
- cabinet departments and what they are
- why so much governing happens through agencies rather than Congress directly

#### Agencies and departments

- what the FDA does
- what the CDC does
- what the EPA does
- what the DOJ does
- what the Treasury does
- what the State Department does
- what the Department of Defense does
- what Homeland Security does
- what NOAA does
- what the SEC does
- what the FTC does
- what the Federal Reserve does versus what Treasury does

#### Landmark policies, acts, and programs

- what the Affordable Care Act did at a high level
- what Social Security does
- what Medicare and Medicaid are and how they differ
- what the Civil Rights Act of 1964 did
- what the Voting Rights Act did
- what the GI Bill did
- what the Federal Reserve Act created at a high level

#### Rights and limits

- what the First Amendment protects and does not protect
- what the Fourth Amendment is about at a practical level
- what due process means at a high level
- what equal protection means at a high level

### Question writer notes

- Best questions often hinge on role, limit, process, policy purpose, or common misconception
- Agency questions should test functional understanding, not acronym memorization alone
- Policy questions should test what problem a policy addressed, what it changed, or how it differed from similar-sounding programs
- Distinction questions work especially well here because many users vaguely recognize agency names or law names without knowing what they actually do
- Avoid current partisan controversy framing; keep it structural and durable
- Good distractors should reflect actual public confusion

---

## B. Economic mechanisms

### Purpose

Help users understand how prices, incentives, inflation, rates, and major macro concepts work.

### What strong questions look like

- ask what changes when rates, prices, or inflation move
- ask how a mechanism transmits through the economy
- ask what a common term really means in practice
- ask what people commonly get wrong
- ask the user to distinguish surface effects from actual underlying mechanics
- ask the user to reason through second-order consequences, not just first-order definitions

### Concept families

#### Core macro mechanisms

- inflation and purchasing power
- interest rates
- what the Federal reserve is and who owns and controls it
- unemployment, recession, and economic slowdown basics
- supply, demand, shortages, and surpluses
- tariffs and tradeoffs

#### Government finance / public finance basics

- deficit vs debt vs budget
- marginal tax brackets vs effective tax rate
- tax withholding vs tax liability vs refund

#### Everyday economics concepts

- opportunity cost
- sunk cost
- real vs nominal values

### Question writer notes

- Do not turn these into soft definitional prompts or obvious misconception bait
- These should often feel one layer harder than the user expects
- Favor questions that test mechanism, tradeoff, transmission, and directional reasoning
- Good distractors should sound plausible to someone with a fuzzy mental model, not obviously wrong to anyone paying attention
- Whenever possible, make the user reason about what follows from a change in one variable or policy rather than just identify the term
- The goal is to expose shallow understanding, not reward test-taking tricks

---

## C. Relationship / variable-change mechanisms

### Purpose

Help users reason through what usually happens when one important variable changes relative to another.

This subtype is powerful because it forces actual understanding rather than memorized definitions.

### What strong questions look like

- ask what generally happens if one variable rises while another stays fixed
- ask which relationship is directionally correct
- ask what outcome is most likely given a change in one parameter

### Concept families

#### Finance / economics relationships

- if interest rates rise, what generally happens to existing bond prices
- if inflation rises faster than wages, what happens to purchasing power
- if you only pay the minimum on high-interest debt, what tends to happen over time
- if a stock price rises while earnings stay flat, what tends to happen to the P/E ratio
- if diversification falls, what tends to happen to concentration risk

#### Government / public structure relationships

- if a law conflicts with the Constitution, which institution may ultimately evaluate that conflict
- if power is shifted from states to the federal government, what kind of dispute is that
- if one branch exceeds its intended role, what constitutional principle is meant to constrain it

### Question writer notes

- These questions should be phrased carefully so they test directional understanding without becoming math-heavy unless intended
- Strong answer choices are often all plausible at first glance
- This lane is especially good for medium and hard questions

---

# Question Lenses

These are not top-level learning roles. They are **ways to frame a question**.

Use one or more when writing questions from the concept families above.

- **timeline**
- **map**
- **officeholder**
- **canonical list**
- **distinction**
- **process**
- **relationship**
- **comparison**
- **misconception correction**
- **application**

### Good examples

- Anchor + timeline lens: “During which years did the Cold War roughly take place?”
- Mechanism + distinction lens: “What is the difference between a marginal tax rate and an effective tax rate?”
- Mechanism + relationship lens: “When market interest rates rise, what usually happens to prices of existing bonds?”
- Anchor + map lens: “Which U.S. state borders both Oregon and Arizona?”

---

# Final guidance for question writers

When using this outline, do not just ask “What is this?”

Ask:

- Is this an anchor or a mechanism?
- What mental action should the user perform?
- What question lens creates the strongest version of this topic?
- What wrong answer choices would fool someone with a weak or fuzzy mental model?
- Can the explanation teach a useful mental model in two sentences or less?

A concept should only stay in the bank if it can support:

- a clean question
- believable distractors
- a short explanation
- a short “why it matters” line
- future expansion from other angles later

That is the standard for Reverb.
